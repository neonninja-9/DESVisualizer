What is DES?

The Data Encryption Standard (DES) is a symmetric-key block cipher that was adopted as a federal standard in the United States in 1977. It encrypts data in fixed 64-bit blocks using a 56-bit key. Although considered insecure today due to its short key length, DES remains one of the most studied and historically significant encryption algorithms. Understanding DES is fundamental to grasping modern cryptographic concepts like Triple DES (3DES) and AES.

Feistel Network Structure

DES is built on a Feistel cipher structure, which means the same algorithm is used for both encryption and decryption (with reversed key order). The 64-bit plaintext block is split into two 32-bit halves ‚Äî the Left (L) and Right (R). In each round, the right half is passed through a round function F and XORed with the left half. Then the halves are swapped. This elegant design ensures that the process is invertible, meaning decryption works by reversing the subkey order.

16 Rounds of Transformation

DES performs 16 rounds of identical operations. In each round:
1. The 32-bit right half is expanded to 48 bits using the Expansion Permutation (E).
2. This is XORed with the round's 48-bit subkey.
3. The result is fed through 8 S-Boxes (Substitution Boxes), reducing it back to 32 bits.
4. A Permutation (P) shuffles the bits.
The output is XORed with the left half, and the halves are swapped for the next round.

Key Schedule

Although DES accepts a 64-bit key, only 56 bits are used (8 bits are parity checks). The key schedule generates 16 subkeys, one for each round. The process begins with Permuted Choice 1 (PC-1), which selects 56 bits and splits them into two 28-bit halves (C and D). These halves are left-shifted by 1 or 2 positions per round. Then Permuted Choice 2 (PC-2) selects 48 bits from the combined 56-bit result to produce each round's subkey.

S-Boxes: The Heart of DES

The 8 S-Boxes are the core non-linear component and the primary source of DES's cryptographic strength. Each S-Box takes a 6-bit input and produces a 4-bit output. The first and last bits determine the row, while the middle four bits determine the column in a lookup table. This non-linear substitution introduces confusion, making it extremely difficult for an attacker to find statistical relationships between the plaintext, ciphertext, and key.

Initial & Final Permutation

Before the 16 rounds begin, the 64-bit plaintext undergoes an Initial Permutation (IP). After all rounds, a Final Permutation (FP or IP‚Åª¬π) is applied ‚Äî which is the exact inverse of the Initial Permutation. These permutations don't add cryptographic strength but were originally designed for hardware implementation efficiency in the 1970s. They rearrange bits in a fixed, predetermined pattern to facilitate parallel processing in hardware.
